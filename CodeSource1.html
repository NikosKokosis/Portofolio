Introduction

This project examines wildfire occurrences in Greece from 2012 to 2016, focusing on patterns in frequency, burned area, and duration across regions. Through data-driven insights, the analysis uncovers key factors impacting wildfire behavior and provides actionable recommendations for disaster preparedness and response.

Key Objectives:

  • Identify high-risk periods and regions.
  • Analyze the correlation between wildfire severity and external factors (e.g., weather, resource allocation).
  • Develop predictive models to anticipate wildfire risks and inform policy decisions.

Wildfire Trends by Year

The annual distribution of wildfires reveals significant fluctuations:

  • 2012: The highest wildfire incidents (24% of total), highlighting critical conditions and increased risk.
  • 2016: A resurgence in wildfire activity (22.2%), reversing the decline seen in 2014–2015.
  • 2014–2015: Notable reduction, potentially due to preventive measures implemented after 2012.

Insights: Consistent monitoring and intervention strategies are crucial to sustaining reductions in wildfire incidents. The cyclical nature of wildfire trends underscores the need for long-term solutions.

Seasonal Trends in Wildfire Incidents

The temporal distribution of wildfires demonstrates strong seasonal patterns:

  • Peak incidents occur during July and August, with August consistently exhibiting the highest numbers.
  • Spring and autumn months show lower activity, though isolated spikes occur in October (notably in the islands).

Insights: Resource allocation should prioritize summer months, especially August, to address peak demand. The October spikes suggest a need for regional-specific preparedness plans.

Geospatial Patterns of Wildfires

Spatial analysis highlights wildfire hotspots:

  • Central Greece: Consistently high wildfire incidents and burned land.
  • Thessaly: Extensive damage in short durations, indicating response inefficiencies.
  • Northern Aegean Islands: Increased activity in autumn, particularly October.

Insights: High-risk regions like Central Greece and Thessaly require improved response strategies. Effective containment in the Ionian Islands demonstrates best practices that can be replicated.

Duration and Severity of Wildfires

Fire duration and severity were assessed across regions:

  • Thessaly: High burned areas in short durations suggest delays in containment measures.
  • Sterea Ellada: Prolonged durations but less burned area compared to Thessaly, indicating better fire management.
  • Peloponnese: Moderate burned areas despite long durations, reflecting effective resource use.

Insights: Rapid response strategies are essential for regions like Thessaly to reduce burned areas. The Peloponnese's resource efficiency should be studied and adopted in other regions.

Correlation Between Wildfires and External Factors

A correlation matrix was used to examine relationships between wildfire severity and influencing factors:

  • Weather Conditions: Minimal impact; most wildfires occurred during predictable summer weather.
  • Human Resources: Firefighter deployment (moderate correlation) significantly reduced fire durations. Aerial support (helicopters, planes) was particularly effective in minimizing burned areas.

Insights: Investment in human and aerial resources yields measurable benefits in reducing wildfire severity. Weather’s limited influence reinforces the importance of proactive firefighting measures.

Predictive Modeling for Wildfire Risk

The innovative metric Burned Area per Minute was developed to predict high-risk periods:

  • July and August 2017: Forecasted spikes in wildfire activity, with burned areas expected to peak at 442.5 hectares/minute.
  • Larissa: Emerged as the highest-risk region, requiring focused monitoring and resource allocation.

Insights: Predictive analytics enables efficient resource allocation during critical periods. High-risk regions like Larissa should serve as priority zones for disaster preparedness.

Recommendations and Policy Insights

Resource allocation and policy recommendations were based on observed trends:

  • Thessaly: Improve rapid response strategies to minimize extensive damage.
  • Central Greece: Increase aerial and ground resources to address prolonged fire durations.
  • Island Regions: Address resource shortages to mitigate risks in autumn months.

Recommendations:

  1. Prioritize human resource recruitment during peak months.
  2. Enhance aerial support in high-risk regions.
  3. Replicate effective containment practices from Ionian Islands.

Conclusion

This analysis demonstrates the power of data-driven strategies in managing wildfires. By identifying patterns and forecasting risks, it provides a blueprint for improving preparedness, safeguarding communities, and protecting ecosystems.
